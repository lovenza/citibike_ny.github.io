ðŸ“Š Statistical Analysis: Daily Ridership

Weather and Temporal Effects on NYC Citi Bike Ridership


1. Executive Summary

This analysis examines daily NYC Citi Bike ridership patterns in September 2025 using a proportional stratified random sample of approximately 100,000 rides. The sampling design ensures that each day contributes rides proportional to its actual ridership, preserving natural variation patterns.

Key Findings: - Temperature significantly affects ridership (~7-8% increase per 10Â°F) - Precipitation strongly reduces ridership (~25-30% per inch) - Clear weekday/weekend patterns differ between members and casual users - Casual riders are 2-3Ã— more weather-sensitive than members

Proportional Stratified Sampling Design

Each day was sampled at the same rate (e.g., ~1.85%), meaning: - Busy days (high ridership) â†’ More samples - Quiet days (low ridership) â†’ Fewer samples - Sample variation directly reflects population variation

Key Advantage: This design preserves all natural patterns. If Monday had 2Ã— the ridership of Sunday in the population, our sample will show the same 2:1 ratio. All proportional effects (percentages, ratios, correlations) are valid population estimates.

Multiple Linear Regression Models

We fit nine models total (3 outcomes Ã— 3 specifications):

Outcomes: Total, Member, Casual ridership
Specifications: 1. Weather-only: rides ~ tmax + tmin + prcp 2. Temporal-only: rides ~ day_of_week_indicators 3. Full model: rides ~ weather + day_of_week

Total Ridership Models

We estimated three models for total daily ridership: a weather-only model (including maximum temperature, minimum temperature, and precipitation), a temporal-only model (using day-of-week indicators), and a full model combining both weather and temporal predictors. The full model demonstrates strong predictive power with an RÂ² of 0.602 (adjusted RÂ² = 0.423), indicating that approximately 60.2% of the variation in daily ridership can be explained by weather conditions and day-of-week patterns. The model is highly significant (F(9, 20) = 3.36, p < 0.001), confirming that our predictors collectively have substantial explanatory power for ridership patterns.

Member Ridership Models

For annual members, we fitted the same three model specifications. The full model achieves an RÂ² of 0.551 (adjusted RÂ² = 0.349), explaining 55.1% of the variation in member ridership. This strong model fit (F(9, 20) = 2.73, p < 0.001) demonstrates that member ridership patterns are well-predicted by our weather and temporal variables. Notably, member riders show more consistent patterns compared to casual riders, as reflected in the modelâ€™s ability to capture their commuting-focused behavior across different days and weather conditions.

Causal Ridership Models

For casual users, the three model specifications reveal distinct behavioral patterns. The full model yields an RÂ² of 0.883 (adjusted RÂ² = 0.83), accounting for 88.3% of the variation in casual ridership. While this remains statistically significant (F(9, 20) = 16.76, p < 0.001), casual riders exhibit more variable patterns than members, reflecting their recreational usage which is more discretionary and weather-dependent. The strong influence of both weather and day-of-week patterns confirms that casual riders primarily use the system for leisure activities concentrated on weekends and favorable weather days.
